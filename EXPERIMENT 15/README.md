# Experiment 15: Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK)

## I. Objectives

The objectives of this experiment are:

1. To understand the principle of **Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK)** as a digital modulation technique.
2. To generate an ASK signal using a digital data source and carrier signal.
3. To observe the ASK waveform using an oscilloscope.
4. To demodulate the ASK signal using an **envelope detector**.
5. To restore the recovered digital signal using a **comparator circuit**.

---

# II. Materials and Components Used

The following equipment and components were used:

- Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 (plus power pack)
- Dual-channel 20 MHz oscilloscope
- Three Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 oscilloscope leads
- Assorted Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 patch leads

---

# III. Procedure

## Part A – Generating an ASK Signal

1. Gather all the required equipment and components.
2. Set up the oscilloscope according to the instructions in the previous experiment.
3. Set the oscilloscope **Trigger Source control to EXT position**.
4. Set the oscilloscope **Trigger Source Coupling control to the HF REJ position**.
5. Set the oscilloscope **Channel 1 and Channel 2 Input Coupling controls to the DC position**.
6. Set the oscilloscope **Timebase control to the mid position**.
7. Connect the setup as shown in the ASK signal generation diagram.
8. Observe the **Sequence Generator module output** and the **ASK signal output** on the oscilloscope.
9. Compare the signals.

---

## Part B – Demodulating an ASK Signal Using an Envelope Detector

1. Modify the setup to include an **envelope detector**.
2. Locate the **Tunable Low Pass Filter module**.
3. Turn the **Gain control fully clockwise**.
4. Turn the **Cut-off Frequency Adjust control fully clockwise**.
5. Connect the setup according to the ASK demodulation block diagram.
6. Observe the **demodulated ASK signal** using the oscilloscope.
7. Compare the recovered signal with the original digital signal.

---

## Part C – Restoring the Recovered Digital Signal Using a Comparator

1. Modify the setup to include a **comparator module**.
2. Connect the **envelope detector output** to the **comparator input**.
3. Adjust the **Variable DC control to the middle position**.
4. Observe the comparator output on the oscilloscope.
5. Compare the **original digital signal** with the **restored digital signal**.
6. Adjust the comparator threshold if necessary.

---

# IV. Results and Discussion

## Part A – Generating an ASK Signal

In this part of the experiment, an **Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK)** signal was generated by controlling a carrier signal using a digital signal from the Sequence Generator module. The Dual Analog Switch module allowed the carrier signal to pass only when the digital input was logic high.

When the digital signal was **logic 1**, the carrier signal appeared at the output as a sinusoidal waveform. When the digital signal was **logic 0**, the carrier signal was blocked and the output voltage became approximately **0 V**.

This produced bursts of sinusoidal signals corresponding to the digital data stream.

### Answers

**What is the ASK signal’s voltage when the digital signal is logic-0?**

The ASK signal voltage is approximately **0 V** when the digital signal is logic-0 because the carrier is switched off.

**What feature of the ASK signal suggests that it’s an AM signal?**

The ASK waveform shows bursts of a sinusoidal carrier whose amplitude changes depending on the digital signal. This behavior is similar to **Amplitude Modulation (AM)**.

---

## Part B – Demodulating an ASK Signal Using an Envelope Detector

In this part, the ASK signal was demodulated using an **envelope detector**, which consisted of a rectifier and a low-pass filter. The envelope detector extracts the amplitude variations of the ASK signal, which correspond to the original digital information.

The recovered waveform resembled the original digital signal, but it did not have sharp transitions. Instead, the waveform showed **slow rising and falling edges**.

### Answers

**Why is the recovered digital signal not a perfect copy of the original?**

The recovered signal is not perfect because of:

- Filtering effects in the envelope detector
- Noise in the communication channel
- Limited response of the circuit components

These factors cause distortion and slow transitions in the signal.

**What can be used to “clean up” the recovered digital signal?**

A **comparator** can be used to clean up the recovered digital signal.

---

## Part C – Restoring the Recovered Digital Signal Using a Comparator

In the final stage of the experiment, a **comparator** was used to restore the digital signal. The comparator compares the recovered analog signal with a reference voltage (threshold level).

When the input signal exceeds the reference voltage, the output becomes **logic high**. When the signal falls below the reference level, the output becomes **logic low**.

This converts the distorted waveform into a **clean digital signal with sharp transitions**.

### Answer

**How does the comparator turn the slow-rising voltages of the recovered digital signal into sharp transitions?**

The comparator compares the signal with a reference voltage. Once the signal crosses the threshold, the comparator switches rapidly between high and low output states, producing a digital waveform with sharp edges.

---

# V. Reflection / Learning Summary

This experiment demonstrated how **Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK)** can be used to transmit digital information using a carrier signal. It showed that the presence or absence of the carrier represents digital data.

Through this experiment, the following concepts were learned:

- ASK modulation works by switching a carrier signal on and off according to digital data.
- ASK is related to **amplitude modulation**, since the carrier amplitude changes based on the data signal.
- Envelope detectors can recover the information from an ASK signal.
- The recovered signal may contain distortions due to filtering and noise.
- Comparators can restore distorted signals into clean digital waveforms.

Overall, the experiment improved understanding of digital communication systems and the processes involved in **signal generation, demodulation, and signal restoration**.

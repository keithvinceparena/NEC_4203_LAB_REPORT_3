# EXPERIMENT 17: BINARY PHASE SHIFT KEYING (BPSK)

## I. OBJECTIVES

The objectives of this experiment are:

1. To understand the concept and operation of **Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK)** as a digital modulation technique.
2. To generate a **BPSK signal** using a digital data stream and a sinusoidal carrier.
3. To observe how the **phase of the carrier signal changes according to the digital data**.
4. To demodulate the BPSK signal using a **product detector**.
5. To restore the recovered digital signal using a **comparator circuit**.

BPSK is a digital modulation scheme where the phase of a carrier signal is shifted by **180° depending on the binary input data**. 

---

# II. MATERIALS AND COMPONENTS USED

The following equipment and components were used in this experiment:

- Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 (plus power pack)
- Dual-channel 20 MHz oscilloscope
- Three Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 oscilloscope leads
- Assorted Emona Telecoms-Trainer 101 patch leads

These instruments were used to generate the BPSK signal, observe its characteristics, and analyze the demodulated output. 

---

# III. PROCEDURE

## Part A – Generating a BPSK Signal

1. Gather all equipment listed in the materials section.
2. Set up the oscilloscope according to the instructions used in the previous experiments.
3. Set the oscilloscope trigger source to **EXT**.
4. Set the oscilloscope trigger coupling to **HF Reject**.
5. Set both **Channel 1 and Channel 2 input coupling to DC**.
6. Adjust the oscilloscope time base control to the middle position.
7. Locate the **Sequence Generator module** and set its **DIP switches to 00** to generate a digital data stream.
8. Locate the **Master Signals module** and use the **100 kHz carrier signal**.
9. Connect the modules according to the **BPSK Signal Generator Block Diagram** shown in the manual.
10. Connect the digital signal output to **Channel 1 of the oscilloscope**.
11. Connect the BPSK signal output from the multiplier module to **Channel 2 of the oscilloscope**.
12. Turn on the trainer and observe the digital data and the BPSK waveform on the oscilloscope.

The BPSK signal is generated by multiplying the digital data signal with the carrier signal using the **multiplier module**. 

---

## Part B – Demodulating a BPSK Signal Using a Product Detector

1. Modify the previous setup by adding another **Multiplier module** and a **Tunable Low Pass Filter (LPF)**.
2. Connect the BPSK signal to the **input of the product detector**.
3. Use the same carrier signal as the **local carrier** for the product detector.
4. Multiply the BPSK signal with the local carrier using the multiplier module.
5. Pass the resulting signal through the **Tunable Low Pass Filter**.
6. Observe the demodulated signal on the oscilloscope.
7. Compare the recovered digital signal with the original digital signal.

This process demodulates the BPSK signal using **product detection**. 

---

## Part C – Restoring the Recovered Data Using a Comparator

1. Modify the setup by adding a **Comparator module**.
2. Connect the output of the Tunable LPF to the comparator input.
3. Set the **Variable DC control** on the comparator module to approximately the midpoint.
4. Observe the comparator output on the oscilloscope.
5. Compare the restored digital signal with the original digital signal.
6. Adjust the reference voltage slightly if necessary to produce a clean digital waveform.

The comparator is used to convert the distorted recovered signal into a clean digital signal.

---

# IV. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

## Part A – Generating a BPSK Signal

When the circuit was assembled, the Sequence Generator produced a digital signal consisting of logic-1 and logic-0 states. This digital signal controlled the multiplication of the carrier signal in the multiplier module.

When the digital signal was **logic-1**, the carrier signal passed through without phase change.  
When the digital signal changed to **logic-0**, the carrier signal was inverted, resulting in a **180° phase shift**.

Thus, the output waveform maintained the same amplitude and frequency but changed phase depending on the binary input. This phase reversal represents the basic principle of **Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK)**.

### Question 1  
**What happens to the BPSK signal on the data stream’s logic transitions?**

When the digital data changes from logic-1 to logic-0 (or vice versa), the phase of the carrier signal shifts by **180 degrees**. This sudden phase reversal represents the binary information being transmitted.

### Question 2  
**What feature of the BPSK signal suggests that it’s a DSBSC signal?**

The BPSK signal has **constant amplitude and no carrier component present**, which indicates that it behaves similarly to a **Double Sideband Suppressed Carrier (DSBSC)** signal. The modulation occurs by multiplying the carrier with the digital signal, suppressing the carrier and leaving only the sideband components.

---

## Part B – Demodulating the BPSK Signal

In this stage, the BPSK signal was demodulated using a **product detector**. The incoming BPSK signal was multiplied by a synchronized local carrier signal. The multiplication process produced components at different frequencies.

The **Tunable Low Pass Filter** was then used to remove the high-frequency components, leaving the baseband digital signal.

Although the recovered signal resembled the original digital signal, it was not perfectly clean because of filtering effects and noise in the system.

### Question 3  
**Why is the recovered digital signal not a perfect copy of the original?**

The recovered signal is not perfect due to several factors:

- Noise in the communication system
- Imperfections in the filtering process
- Limited bandwidth of the components
- Distortion introduced by the demodulation process

These factors cause the recovered waveform to have **rounded edges and slight distortion**, rather than sharp digital transitions.

### Question 4  
**What can be used to “clean up” the recovered signal?**

A **Comparator circuit** can be used to clean up the recovered signal.  
The comparator converts the distorted analog waveform into a clean digital signal by comparing it with a reference voltage.

---

## Part C – Restoring the Recovered Data

After demodulation, the recovered digital signal contained slow rising and falling edges due to filtering and distortion. To restore the signal, a comparator module was used.

The comparator compares the input signal with a reference voltage. When the input signal exceeds the reference voltage, the comparator outputs a **logic high**, and when it falls below the threshold, the output becomes **logic low**.

This process restores the signal into a clean digital square waveform.

### Question 5  
**Why does varying the DC voltage on the comparator’s input change the shape of the digital signal?**

The DC voltage sets the **threshold level** for the comparator. Changing this voltage shifts the point at which the comparator switches between high and low outputs.

If the threshold is too high or too low, the comparator may trigger too early or too late, causing distortion in the recovered digital waveform.

Therefore, adjusting the reference voltage changes the timing and shape of the comparator’s output signal.

# V. REFLECTION / LEARNING SUMMARY

This experiment demonstrated the operation of **Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK)**, a digital modulation technique widely used in modern communication systems.

Through the experiment, the following concepts were understood:

- Digital data can be transmitted by changing the **phase of a carrier signal**.
- BPSK represents binary data by shifting the carrier phase by **180 degrees**.
- The BPSK signal resembles a **DSBSC signal** due to the suppression of the carrier.
- Product detection can be used to demodulate BPSK signals.
- Comparator circuits help restore distorted analog signals into clean digital signals.

The experiment provided practical experience in generating, observing, demodulating, and restoring BPSK signals using communication system components. Understanding BPSK is important because it forms the foundation for more advanced modulation schemes such as **QPSK and other digital communication techniques**.
